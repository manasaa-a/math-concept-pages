What is Surface Area of a Cylinder?

Think about it

Imagine cutting along the height of a soup can and flattening it out. What shape do you get? What are its dimensions?

A right circular cylinder with radius r and height h

The surface area of a cylinder refers to the total area that the cylinder's curved surface and circular bases cover. In other words: it is the total area covered by its two circular bases and its curved lateral surface.

This area splits naturally into two pieces:

  • Total Surface Area (TSA) — both circular bases plus the curved face. This is what you'd need if you were sealing every face of the cylinder.
  • Curved Surface Area (CSA) — only the curved lateral surface. This is the area of the "label" wrapped around the side.
Mini check

A cylinder has r = 5 cm and h = 12 cm. Which surface area — TSA or CSA — would a label manufacturer care about?

The label wraps around the side only, not the tops. So it's the curved surface area (CSA).

Formulas

There are three formulas to know for cylinders — pick the one that matches what you're measuring.

Curved Surface Area (CSA)
CSA = 2πrh

Derivation. Imagine cutting the cylinder vertically along its height and unrolling it. The curved face becomes a rectangle. Its height is just h, and its width equals the circumference of the base — that is, 2πr. So the area of that rectangle is 2πr × h.

Unrolling the curved face gives a rectangle of width 2πr and height h
Worked example

r = 7 cm, h = 14 cm. Using π = 22/7:

CSA = 2 × (22/7) × 7 × 14 = 2 × 22 × 14 = 616 cm²

Total Surface Area (TSA)
TSA = 2πr² + 2πrh = 2πr(r + h)

The total surface is two circular bases (area πr² each, so 2πr² together) plus the curved face (2πrh). Factor out 2πr and you get the compact form 2πr(r + h).

Worked example

r = 5 in, h = 15 in:

TSA = 2π(5)(5 + 15) = 2π(5)(20) = 200π ≈ 628 sq in

Try in simulation →
Open Cylinder (no top)
TSAopen = πr² + 2πrh = πr(r + 2h)

Some cylinders have only one base — think open tanks, bowls, or buckets. The formula keeps the curved face (2πrh) but counts the base only once (πr²).

Worked example

An open cylindrical bucket has r = 7 cm, h = 10 cm. Using π = 22/7:

TSAopen = (22/7) × 7 × (7 + 20) = 22 × 27 = 594 cm²

Key Takeaways

Cylinder
A 3D solid with two congruent circular bases connected by a curved lateral surface.
Radius (r)
The distance from the centre of a circular base to its edge.
Height (h)
The perpendicular distance between the two circular bases.
Curved Surface Area (CSA)
The area of only the curved lateral face: 2πrh. Equals the area of the rectangle you get when you unroll the side.
Total Surface Area (TSA)
CSA plus both circular bases: 2πr(r + h). What you need to coat or cover the whole cylinder.
Open Cylinder
A cylinder missing one base (e.g. a bucket). TSA = πr(r + 2h).
Net of a Cylinder
The 2D shape you get by unrolling — one rectangle (2πr by h) plus two circles (radius r).
π (Pi)
The ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. Approximately 22/7 or 3.14159.

FAQ

What is the surface area of a cylinder?

The surface area of a cylinder refers to the total area that the cylinder's curved surface and circular bases cover. It is measured in square units and computed using the formula 2πr(r + h), where r is the radius and h is the height.

What is the difference between TSA and CSA of a cylinder?

TSA (Total Surface Area) includes both the circular bases and the curved lateral face — formula 2πr(r + h). CSA (Curved Surface Area) is only the curved lateral face — formula 2πrh. Use CSA when you only care about the "label" area; use TSA when you need every face, including the top and bottom discs.

How do you find surface area when the diameter is given?

Halve the diameter to get the radius (r = d/2), then plug r into the standard formula 2πr(r + h). For example, if d = 14 cm and h = 10 cm, then r = 7 cm, and TSA = 2π(7)(7 + 10) = 238π cm².

What is the surface area of an open cylinder?

For a cylinder open at one end (one base missing), the surface area is πr² + 2πrh, which factors as πr(r + 2h). This applies to objects like open buckets, paper cups, and topless tanks.

Why does the curved surface area formula use 2πr × h?

If you cut the cylinder along its height and unroll the curved face, you get a rectangle. Its width equals the circumference of the base (2πr), and its height is just h. So the area is width × height = 2πr × h.

Can the curved surface area be zero?

Only if r = 0 or h = 0 — both of which make the cylinder degenerate. If r = 0, the cylinder collapses to a line. If h = 0, it collapses to a flat disc. Neither is a real cylinder, so for any genuine cylinder CSA is strictly positive.

What are common mistakes when calculating cylinder surface area?

The three biggest ones: (1) forgetting to double the base area — TSA uses 2πr², not πr², because there are two bases; (2) using diameter instead of radius — always halve the diameter first; (3) confusing CSA and TSA — re-read the problem and check whether the question is about a label (CSA) or the whole shell (TSA).
