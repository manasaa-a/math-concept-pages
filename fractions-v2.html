Fractions · Grades 3–5

The first time you said "half" you were already doing fractions — you just didn't have a way to write it down yet.

Introduction · Grades 3–5

When something gets split into equal parts, whole numbers stop being enough. You need a way to write part of something. That's all a fraction is — a precise way to say how much of a whole you have.

38

🍕
Sharing a pizza
Eight friends, one pizza — each person gets 1/8.
🥄
Following a recipe
A cake needs 3/4 cup of flour — three out of four equal parts.
💰
Splitting a bill
Three friends share a $24 lunch. Each pays 1/3.
⏰
Telling time
Half past three is 1/2 of an hour past 3.
By the end of this page, you'll see exactly how to write "three slices out of eight" as 3/8 — and why that simple notation is more powerful than it first looks.
Building the idea

A fraction tells you "how much, out of a whole"

Start with one whole thing — a pizza. Cut it into equal parts. The number of equal parts is called the denominator.

This pizza has four equal parts. So the denominator is 4.

Quick check
A pizza is cut into 6 equal slices. What's the denominator?

Now take some of those equal parts. The number you take is called the numerator.

Three slices taken out of four. That's three-fourths, or 3/4.

Put them together with a bar. The number on top counts what you have. The number below counts the equal parts the whole is split into.

Remember the pizza we started with — three slices taken out of eight? That's exactly 3/8. You already had the idea. Now you have the notation to write it down.
