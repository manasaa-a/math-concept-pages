Area of a Circle · Grades 6–8

From a coin to a clock to a planet, every circle shares one number that decides its size.

Introduction · Grades 6–8

Rectangles are easy — length × width. Triangles are half of that. But round things have no straight sides at all, so neither trick works. There has to be a way — and once you've seen it, you'll never forget the formula.

Now formalise it

Why the rectangle is exactly πr × r

You saw it: the wedges flatten into a rectangle. Two facts make the formula click.

Height = r. Each wedge had its tip at the centre and its base on the rim. When you stand a wedge on its base, its height is the radius — the centre-to-rim distance.

Width = πr. The wedges have flat-side bases on top and bottom of the rectangle. The bottom row's bases were originally on the rim, so they add up to half the circumference = (1/2)(2πr) = πr.

Area = πr × r = πr2 because area of a rectangle is just width × height
Remember the cake we started with — area = "?". Now the question mark has a name: πr². And π isn't a magic number — it's the ratio that makes the unrolled cake's width work out exactly.
