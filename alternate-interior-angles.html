Alternate Interior Angles

By the end of this page, you'll understand alternate interior angles — how to identify them, why they're always equal when lines are parallel, and how they differ from co-interior angles.

Two parallel streets, one diagonal road — the angles inside the streets on opposite sides are always identical.

Think about it

Maple Street and Oak Street are parallel. Fifth Avenue crosses both at an angle, making a 55° angle on the left side between the streets at Maple. What angle does it make on the right side between the streets at Oak — and why are they equal?

Alternate interior angles are angle pairs formed when a transversal crosses two lines. They have two defining features: they lie between the two lines (interior) and on opposite sides of the transversal (alternate). When the lines are parallel, alternate interior angles are always equal.

They are easy to spot in a diagram — they form a Z-shape or N-shape with the transversal and the two lines.

Pair 1∠3 and ∠6Between the lines, opposite sides of the transversal

Alternate Interior Angles Theorem

The theorem ties alternate interior angles to parallel lines — and works in both directions.

Theorem
If a transversal crosses two parallel lines, alternate interior angles are congruent (equal).

The two angles form a Z or N shape — opposite sides of the transversal, between the lines. The parallel structure forces them equal. This can be proved from corresponding angles and vertical angles.

Example 1
Lines ∥, ∠3 = 65° → ∠6 = 65°
Example 2
Lines ∥, ∠4 = 48° → ∠5 = 48°
Converse
If alternate interior angles are equal, the two lines are parallel.

Used in geometry proofs: demonstrating that alternate interior angles are equal is a valid method to prove two lines are parallel without directly comparing their orientation.

Example
∠3 = ∠6 = 70° → lines are parallel ✓
Example
∠4 = ∠5 = 110° → lines are parallel ✓
Co-interior (same-side interior) angles
When lines are parallel: co-interior angles sum to 180° — NOT equal.

Co-interior angles are both interior but on the same side of the transversal. They are supplementary when lines are parallel. Don't confuse them with alternate interior angles.

Alternate interior
∠3 and ∠6 — equal (opposite sides)
Co-interior
∠3 and ∠5 — sum = 180° (same side)

If ∠3 = 65°, then ∠6 = 65° (alternate) but ∠5 = 115° (co-interior).

Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. ∠4 = 75°. What is ∠5, the alternate interior angle?
Alternate interior angles are equal when lines are parallel.
Question 2 of 5
Two parallel streets are crossed by a diagonal avenue. It makes a 48° angle on the left side inside the streets at the first crossing. What angle does it make on the right side inside the streets at the second crossing?
These are alternate interior angles — between the lines, on opposite sides of the avenue. They are equal.
Question 3 of 5
Two alternate interior angles measure (3x + 15)° and 60°. Find x.
Alternate interior angles are equal. Set 3x + 15 = 60 and solve.
Question 4 of 5
A transversal crosses two lines. The co-interior angles on one side measure 65° and 115°. What does this tell you about the two lines?
Co-interior angles sum to 180° when lines are parallel. Do 65° + 115° = 180°?
Question 5 of 5
If ∠3 = (7x − 4)° and ∠6 = (5x + 16)°, for what value of x are the two lines parallel?
Lines are parallel when alternate interior angles are equal. Set 7x − 4 = 5x + 16 and solve.

Key Takeaways

TermDefinition
Alternate Interior AnglesAngle pairs between the two lines, on opposite sides of the transversal. Equal when lines are parallel.
InteriorBetween the two lines crossed by the transversal — not outside them.
AlternateOn opposite sides of the transversal — one left, one right.
The 2 Pairs∠3 and ∠6 (Pair 1); ∠4 and ∠5 (Pair 2). Standard 1–8 numbering with top intersection 1–4, bottom 5–8.
Co-interior AnglesSame-side interior angles. Supplementary (sum = 180°) when lines are parallel. Not equal like alternate interior angles.

Frequently Asked Questions

Angle pairs formed by a transversal crossing two lines, located between the lines and on opposite sides of the transversal. Equal when lines are parallel.
Only when the two lines are parallel. On non-parallel lines, alternate interior angles are not necessarily equal.
Alternate interior: both between the lines, opposite sides of transversal. Corresponding: same side of transversal, one interior and one exterior. Both pairs are equal when lines are parallel.
When a transversal crosses two parallel lines, alternate interior angles are congruent. This is provable from corresponding angles and vertical angles.
Also called same-side interior or consecutive interior angles. Both interior but on the SAME side of the transversal. They are supplementary (sum = 180°) when lines are parallel — not equal.
By the converse: if alternate interior angles are equal, the lines must be parallel. Showing equal alternate interior angles is a valid geometry proof technique for parallelism.
Two pairs: ∠3 and ∠6 (Pair 1), and ∠4 and ∠5 (Pair 2). These are the four interior angles — two at each intersection.
A visual shortcut: alternate interior angles form a Z-shape or N-shape in diagrams. The two arms of the Z lie along the parallel lines, and the diagonal is the transversal — the angles are at the corners of the Z.
