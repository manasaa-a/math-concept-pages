Fractions · Grades 3–5

You've said "half" a hundred times — but what does it actually mean, in numbers?

Introduction · Grades 3–5

When you share something equally, whole numbers stop being enough. There's no whole number for "half" — so mathematicians invented a new kind of number. Let's see exactly what it is and how to write it.

38
three slices, out of eight equal parts
🍕
Sharing a pizza
Eight friends, one pizza — each person gets 1/8.
🥄
Following a recipe
A cake needs 3/4 cup of flour — 3 out of 4 equal parts.
💰
Splitting a bill
Three friends share a meal equally — each pays 1/3.
⏰
Telling time
Half past three is 1/2 of an hour past three.
By the end of this page, you'll see exactly how to write "three slices out of eight" as 3/8 — and why that simple notation is more powerful than it first looks.
Building the idea

Let's take it one step at a time

Think about it

If you cut a pizza into 4 slices versus 8 slices, the pieces look different sizes. What exactly makes them different?

Start with one whole pizza. Now cut it into equal parts. Notice that the number of equal parts you create determines how big each piece is — more cuts means smaller slices.

That number — how many equal parts the whole is split into — has a name. It's called the denominator.

Can you see why it's so important? Without knowing the denominator, you don't know how big a "piece" is.

Quick check
A pizza is cut into 6 equal slices. What is the denominator?
The denominator counts equal parts. Count the slices in the diagram.
Think about it

You know how many equal parts the pizza has. But how do you say how many of those parts you actually have?

Now take some of those equal parts. The number you take — or the number you're counting — is called the numerator.

Three slices taken from a pizza cut into four. That's three-fourths — written as 3/4. The numerator (3) sits on top, the denominator (4) sits below.

Quick check
This pizza shows...
Count the filled slices for the top number, total slices for the bottom.

Put them together with a bar between them. The number on top counts what you have. The number below counts the equal parts the whole is split into.

You read 3/8 as "three eighths" — three out of eight equal parts. That's all a fraction ever is.

Remember the pizza we started with — 3 slices out of 8? That's exactly 3/8. You already had the idea. Now you have the notation to write it anywhere.

Going further

The same idea, three different forms

Once you can write fractions, you'll meet them in three flavours. Notice that they all mean the same kind of thing.

Think about it

What happens if you take more slices than a pizza has? Or if you have a whole pizza and a few extra slices from another one? How would you write that?

34

Proper fraction

Numerator is smaller than denominator. You have less than one whole.
54

Improper fraction

Numerator is larger than denominator. You have more than one whole.
114

Mixed number

A whole number with a proper fraction attached. Same value as 5/4.

Here's something worth pausing on:

Yes — they're equivalent fractions. Different numbers, but the exact same amount of pizza. Notice the pattern: each time you double both the numerator and denominator, the value stays the same.
