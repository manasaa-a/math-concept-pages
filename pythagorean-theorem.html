Pythagorean Theorem · Grades 6–8

Cut across a rectangular field instead of walking around it. The shortcut has a hidden formula.

Introduction · Grades 6–8

Walk along the edges of a rectangle and you take the long way. Walk diagonally, and the corner-to-corner distance follows a strict rule. That rule — the Pythagorean Theorem — works for every right triangle in the universe.

a2 + b2 = c2
two short sides, squared, add up to the long side, squared
🪜
Ladder against a wall
A 13-ft ladder leaning 5 ft from the wall reaches 12 ft up — exactly.
📺
TV diagonal
A "55-inch TV" is 55 inches corner to corner — a Pythagoras measurement.
⚾
Baseball diamond
First base to third is the diagonal — 90√2 ≈ 127 ft.
📍
GPS shortcuts
"As the crow flies" between two streets is always a Pythagorean shortcut.
By the end of this page, you'll see why the two short sides squared have to add up to the long side squared — not by memorising, but by watching the squares change live.

Now name it

The rule that works for every right triangle

The two short sides are called the legs. The longest side, opposite the right angle, is the hypotenuse. Square each side, and the two leg squares add up to exactly the hypotenuse square.

a2 + b2 = c2 where a and b are the legs, and c is the hypotenuse
Remember the diagonal-shortcut idea we started with? That diagonal is the hypotenuse. So the shortcut distance is always √(a² + b²) — and now you know exactly why.
