The foundation

What are exponents?

An exponent (also called a power or index) tells you how many times the base is multiplied by itself.

23 = 2 × 2 × 2 = 8
54 = 5 × 5 × 5 × 5 = 625
x1 = x (one copy of x) = x
70 = follow the pattern: 7², 7¹, 7⁰… = 1
Think about it

Look at: 2⁴ = 16, 2³ = 8, 2² = 4, 2¹ = 2. Each step divides by 2. What does the pattern force 2⁰ to be?

All 8 rules

The 8 exponent rules

For rational functions, apply these in order. The first two rules require the same base. The others apply freely.

am × an = am+n

The product rule of exponents is used to multiply expressions with the same bases.

Example: 2³ × 2⁵ = 23+5 = 2⁸ = 256
Note: Only works when the bases are the same.
Count the base factors — m from the first term, n from the second = m+n total.
am ÷ an = am−n

The quotient law of exponents is used to divide expressions with the same bases.

Example: 3⁶ ÷ 3² = 36−2 = 3⁴ = 81
Note: Same base required. If m < n the result is a negative exponent.
The n factors in the denominator cancel n from the numerator, leaving m−n.
a0 = 1  (a ≠ 0)

The zero law of exponents is applied when the exponent of an expression is 0.

Example: 7⁰ = 1  ·  x⁰ = 1  ·  (−3)⁰ = 1
Note: 0⁰ is undefined — this rule only applies when a ≠ 0.
aᵐ ÷ aᵐ = a⁰. Anything divided by itself equals 1. So a⁰ = 1.
a−m = 1/am

The negative law of exponents is used when an exponent is a negative number.

Example: 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8 = 0.125
Note: A negative exponent means reciprocal — not a negative result.
a⁰ ÷ aᵐ = a⁻ᵐ. Since a⁰ = 1, we get a⁻ᵐ = 1/aᵐ.
(am)n = amn

The power of a power law of exponents is used to simplify expressions of the form (aᵐ)ⁿ.

Example: (2³)⁴ = 23×4 = 2¹² = 4096
Common mistake: (2³)⁴ ≠ 2⁷ — multiply the exponents, don't add them.
n copies of aᵐ each give m factors of a — m × n factors total.
(ab)m = ambm

The power of a product rule of exponents is used to find the result of a product that is raised to an exponent.

Example: (2x)³ = 2³ × x³ = 8x³
Example: (3xy)² = 3² × x² × y² = 9x²y²
The exponent distributes to each factor inside the brackets.
(a/b)m = am/bm

The power of a quotient rule of exponents is used to find the result of a quotient that is raised to an exponent.

Example: (x/y)³ = x³/y³
Example: (2/3)⁴ = 2⁴/3⁴ = 16/81
Same as Power of a Product — numerators group together, denominators group together.
am/n = n√(am)

The fractional exponents rule says a1/n = ⁿ√a — a fractional exponent results in a radical.

Example: 82/3 = ³√(8²) = ³√64 = 4
Special case: a1/n = n√a  ·  Example: 161/2 = √16 = 4
Denominator → root index, numerator → power. Fractional exponents and radicals are two ways of writing the same thing.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

The 8 laws are: (1) Product — aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ, (2) Quotient — aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ, (3) Zero — a⁰ = 1, (4) Identity — a¹ = a, (5) Negative — a⁻ᵐ = 1/aᵐ, (6) Power of a power — (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ, (7) Power of a product — (ab)ᵐ = aᵐbᵐ, (8) Power of a quotient — (a/b)ᵐ = aᵐ/bᵐ.
Follow the pattern: 2³ = 8, 2² = 4, 2¹ = 2. Each step divides by 2. Continuing: 2⁰ = 2 ÷ 2 = 1. Also: aᵐ ÷ aᵐ = aᵐ⁻ᵐ = a⁰. But anything divided by itself = 1, so a⁰ = 1. Note: 0⁰ is undefined — this rule only applies when a ≠ 0.
am/n = n√(am). The denominator of the fraction becomes the root index; the numerator becomes the power. Example: 82/3 = ³√(8²) = ³√64 = 4. When the numerator is 1: a1/n = n√a. Example: 161/2 = √16 = 4. Fractional exponents and radicals are two ways of writing the same thing.
A negative exponent means take the reciprocal: a⁻ᵐ = 1/aᵐ. Example: 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8. The negative exponent does not mean the result is negative — it means "divide by" instead of "multiply by". So 2⁻² = 0.25, which is positive.
No. The product rule (aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ) only works when the bases are the same. 2³ × 3⁴ cannot be simplified by adding exponents — these must be calculated separately: 8 × 81 = 648.
(aᵐ)ⁿ is the power of a power — you multiply the exponents: (a²)³ = a⁶. aᵐ × aⁿ is the product rule — you add the exponents: a² × a³ = a⁵. A common mistake is confusing which operation applies. If the exponent is outside brackets, multiply; if it's a multiplication, add.
Yes. A fractional exponent aᵐ/ⁿ = ⁿ√(aᵐ) — the denominator of the fraction becomes the root. Example: 8^(1/3) = ³√8 = 2. And 8^(2/3) = ³√(8²) = ³√64 = 4. This is called the fractional exponent rule, sometimes listed as a 9th law.
Ask these questions in order: (1) Are the bases the same? → Product or Quotient rule. (2) Is there a power outside brackets? → Power of Power, Product, or Quotient. (3) Is the exponent 0? → Zero rule. (4) Is the exponent 1? → Identity rule. (5) Is the exponent negative? → Negative rule. Most complex expressions need two or more rules applied in sequence.
