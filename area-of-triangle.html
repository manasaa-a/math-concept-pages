Area of a Triangle

By the end of this page, you'll be able to find the area of any triangle — no matter what information you're given. Including the base–height formula, Heron's formula for three sides, the SAS formula with an angle, and special cases for equilateral and isosceles triangles.

You already know the area of a rectangle — cut it diagonally, and you've just discovered the triangle formula.

What is the Area of a Triangle?

Think about it

Take any rectangle and cut it diagonally corner to corner. You get two triangles — each one exactly half the rectangle. That's the intuition behind the whole formula.

The area of a triangle is the amount of 2D space enclosed within its three sides, measured in square units (cm², m², in²…).

A triangle is formed by three line segments. Its area always equals half the area of the parallelogram that shares its base and height — which is why every formula has that ½ in it.

Key rule: the height (h) must be perpendicular to the base. It doesn't matter where along the base the height lands — the formula is always the same.

Mini-check

A rectangle is 8 cm wide and 5 cm tall. A diagonal cuts it into two triangles. What is the area of each triangle? You can work it out in your head before scrolling down.

All Formulas at a Glance

The right formula depends on what you know. Here are all six cases:

Base and Height (most common)
A = ½ × b × h
b = base, h = perpendicular height
Three Sides Known — Heron's Formula
A = √[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)]
s = (a+b+c)/2 (semi-perimeter)
Two Sides + Included Angle (SAS)
A = ½ × b × c × sin(A)
b, c = two sides; A = angle between them
Equilateral Triangle
A = (√3 / 4) × a²
a = side length (all sides equal)
Isosceles Triangle
A = ¼b√(4a² − b²)
a = equal sides, b = base
Right Triangle
A = ½ × leg₁ × leg₂
The two legs are already perpendicular

How to Find the Area of a Triangle

The method depends on what information you have. Use the accordion below to find your case.

Formula
A = ½ × base × height

  • Identify the base — any side can be the base.

  • Find the perpendicular height: the distance from the opposite vertex to the base line, measured at a right angle.

  • Multiply base × height, then halve the result.

Example 1
Base = 10 cm, Height = 5 cm
A = ½ × 10 × 5 = 25 cm²
Example 2
Base = 8 cm, Height = 7 cm
A = ½ × 8 × 7 = 28 cm²
Formula
s = (a+b+c)/2  |  A = √[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)]

  • Find the semi-perimeter: s = (a + b + c) ÷ 2.

  • Calculate each factor: (s−a), (s−b), (s−c).

  • Multiply all four: s × (s−a) × (s−b) × (s−c). Take the square root.

Example: sides 5, 6, 7
s = (5+6+7)/2 = 9
A = √[9 × 4 × 3 × 2] = √216 ≈ 14.7 cm²
Formula
A = ½ × b × c × sin(A)

Where b and c are the two known sides, and A is the angle between them.

Example
Sides b = 4, c = 6; included angle A = 30°
A = ½ × 4 × 6 × sin(30°) = 12 × 0.5 = 6 sq units
Formula
A = (√3 / 4) × a²

Derived from the base–height formula: the height of an equilateral triangle with side a is (√3/2)a.

Side = 2 cm
A = (√3/4) × 4 = √3 ≈ 1.73 cm²
Side = 6 cm
A = (√3/4) × 36 = 9√3 ≈ 15.59 cm²
Formula
A = ¼ × b × √(4a² − b²)

Where a = the two equal sides, b = the base. The formula computes the height using Pythagoras first.

Equal sides a = 5, Base b = 6
Height = √(25 − 9) = 4
A = ½ × 6 × 4 = 12 cm²

What is the area of this triangle?
Use A = ½ × base × height. The green dashed line is the height.
Question 2 of 5
A triangle has a base of 10 cm and a perpendicular height of 8 cm. What is its area?
A = ½ × 10 × 8. Multiply first, then halve.
Question 3 of 5
A yacht's triangular sail has a base of 3.2 m and a height of 5.5 m. How many square metres of cloth does the sailmaker need?
A = ½ × 3.2 × 5.5. Work out 3.2 × 5.5 first.
Question 4 of 5
An equilateral triangle has a side length of 6 cm. Which expression gives its area?
The formula is (√3/4) × a². Here a = 6, so a² = 36.
Question 5 of 5
A triangle has an area of 30 cm². Its height is 6 cm. What is the length of its base?
Rearrange A = ½bh: b = (2 × A) ÷ h. Substitute A = 30 and h = 6.

Key Takeaways

Term / FormulaWhat it means
Area of a TriangleThe 2D space enclosed by a triangle's three sides, measured in square units.
A = ½ × b × hThe core formula. Base and perpendicular height must be at 90° to each other.
Perpendicular HeightThe vertical distance from the apex to the base line — not the slant side. This is what makes two triangles with the same base and height equal in area.
Heron's Formula√[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)], where s = (a+b+c)/2. Used when three sides are known but no height is given.
SAS FormulaA = ½ × b × c × sin(A). Used when two sides and the angle between them are known.
Equilateral TriangleA = (√3/4)a². All three sides equal; height is always (√3/2)a.
