Integers

By the end of this page, you'll understand integers — what they are, how all four operations work, and why the sign rules behave the way they do.

What are Integers?

Think about it

You press the lift button for floor −2 (basement). You press floor +5 (fifth floor). You never press floor 2.7 or floor ½. Why not? Because building floors are integers — whole values with no fractions allowed.

An integer is any whole number — positive, negative, or zero — with no fractional or decimal part.

The set of integers is written as ℤ = {…, −4, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, …} — it extends infinitely in both directions. The letter Z comes from the German word Zahlen, meaning "numbers."

Three things to remember: positive integers (1, 2, 3, …) sit to the right of zero; negative integers (−1, −2, −3, …) sit to the left; and zero is an integer — the only one that is neither positive nor negative.

Integers on a Number Line

Every integer has a fixed address on the number line. Numbers increase as you move right and decrease as you move left — making it a perfect tool for addition and subtraction.

To add a positive integer, move right. To add a negative integer (or subtract a positive), move left. The elevator in our hook moves +4 (right to floor 4), then −7 (left by 7 steps), landing at floor −3.

The number line also shows absolute value: the distance from any integer to zero, always positive. The absolute value of −5 is 5; the absolute value of 5 is also 5.

Two rules cover every case:

Same signs → Add and keep the sign
(−9) + (−5) = −14  |  4 + 3 = 7
Different signs → Subtract and take the sign of the larger
2 + (−5) = −3  |  (−2) + 5 = 3
Step-by-step: 2 + (−5)
Absolute values: 2 and 5
Difference: 5 − 2 = 3
Larger (5) is negative → Result: −3
Step-by-step: (−9) + (−5)
Same sign (both −)
Add: 9 + 5 = 14
Keep the sign → Result: −14

Key rule
a − b = a + (−b) — convert to addition, then apply addition rules

Subtracting a negative is the same as adding a positive. This is why two negatives give a positive in subtraction.

Example 1: 7 − 10
= 7 + (−10)
Different signs, difference: 10 − 7 = 3
Larger is negative → Result: −3
Example 2: −4 − (−6)
= −4 + 6
Different signs, difference: 6 − 4 = 2
Larger (6) is positive → Result: +2

The sign of the product depends only on the signs of the factors:

SignsResultExample
(+) × (+)Positive3 × 4 = 12
(+) × (−)Negative3 × (−4) = −12
(−) × (+)Negative(−3) × 4 = −12
(−) × (−)Positive(−3) × (−4) = 12

Why does negative × negative = positive? Think of it as reversing direction twice — two reversals bring you back to the original direction.

Division follows the exact same sign rules as multiplication:

SignsResultExample
(+) ÷ (+)Positive12 ÷ 3 = 4
(+) ÷ (−)Negative12 ÷ (−3) = −4
(−) ÷ (+)Negative(−12) ÷ 3 = −4
(−) ÷ (−)Positive(−12) ÷ (−3) = 4

Note: dividing integers does not always produce an integer (e.g., 5 ÷ 2 = 2.5). Division is only closed over integers when the result is exact.

Properties of Integers

Seven properties define how integers behave under operations. These aren't just rules to memorise — they're the reason mental arithmetic shortcuts work.

Rule
If a ∈ ℤ and b ∈ ℤ, then a + b, a − b, and a × b are all ∈ ℤ

Adding, subtracting, or multiplying two integers always gives another integer. Division is NOT always closed: 5 ÷ 2 = 2.5, which is not an integer.

Example
(−7) + 4 = −3 ✓ still an integer
Not closed for ÷
7 ÷ 2 = 3.5 ✗ not an integer
Rule
a + b = b + a    and    a × b = b × a

Order doesn't matter for addition and multiplication. It does matter for subtraction and division: 5 − 3 ≠ 3 − 5.

Addition
(−4) + 7 = 7 + (−4) = 3
Multiplication
(−3) × 5 = 5 × (−3) = −15
Rule
(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)    and    (a × b) × c = a × (b × c)

Grouping doesn't matter for addition and multiplication. This is why 3 + (7 + 2) = (3 + 7) + 2 = 12 — you can bracket any way you like.

Rule
a × (b + c) = (a × b) + (a × c)

Multiplication distributes over addition. This is the basis for expanding brackets in algebra.

Example
(−3) × (7 + 2) = (−3) × 9 = −27
= (−3)×7 + (−3)×2 = −21 + (−6) = −27 ✓
Rules
Additive identity: a + 0 = a   |   Multiplicative identity: a × 1 = a

Adding 0 leaves any integer unchanged. Multiplying by 1 leaves any integer unchanged. Zero and one are the "do nothing" numbers for their respective operations.

Rule
a + (−a) = 0    (every integer has an opposite that cancels it)

Every integer has an additive inverse — its negative. Gaining $50 and losing $50 leaves you exactly where you started. This is why debts cancel.

Example
7 + (−7) = 0
Example
(−23) + 23 = 0

Key Takeaways

TermDefinition
IntegerAny whole number — positive, negative, or zero — with no fractional or decimal part. Set: ℤ = {…, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, …}.
Positive IntegerAny integer greater than zero (1, 2, 3, …). Sits to the right of zero on the number line.
Negative IntegerAny integer less than zero (−1, −2, −3, …). Sits to the left of zero on the number line.
Absolute ValueThe distance of an integer from zero, always non-negative. |−7| = 7, |4| = 4.
Additive InverseThe opposite of an integer: a + (−a) = 0. Every integer has one.
Sign Rules (×÷)Same signs → positive result. Different signs → negative result.

FAQs on Integers

What are integers in maths?

Integers are all whole numbers — positive, negative, and zero — with no fractional or decimal part. The set is written ℤ = {…, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …}. The name comes from the Latin integer, meaning "whole."

What is the difference between integers and whole numbers?

Whole numbers are {0, 1, 2, 3, …} — zero and positive integers only. Integers include all of those plus all negative integers (−1, −2, −3, …). Every whole number is an integer, but not every integer is a whole number.

How do you add integers with different signs?

Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger. Then apply the sign of the number with the larger absolute value. For example: 3 + (−8) → |8| − |3| = 5, and since 8 > 3 and 8 is negative, the answer is −5.

Why does negative × negative = positive?

Think of a negative sign as "the opposite direction." Multiplying by a negative flips the direction once. Multiplying by two negatives flips it twice — which brings you back to the original (positive) direction. Formally, it follows from the distributive property and the requirement that arithmetic stays consistent.

Is 0 an integer?

Yes. Zero is an integer — it has no decimal or fractional part. It is the only integer that is neither positive nor negative. It is also both a whole number and a natural number (depending on the convention used).

What are the properties of integers?

The seven key properties are: Closure (addition, subtraction, multiplication stay within ℤ), Commutative (order doesn't affect + and ×), Associative (grouping doesn't affect + and ×), Distributive (× distributes over +), Additive Identity (a + 0 = a), Multiplicative Identity (a × 1 = a), and Additive Inverse (a + (−a) = 0).

Why is the set of integers called ℤ?

ℤ comes from the German word Zahlen, meaning "numbers." German mathematicians were among the first to formalise the theory of integers in the 19th century, and the notation stuck in international mathematical writing.

Can integers be fractions or decimals?

No. The defining feature of an integer is that it has no fractional or decimal component. Numbers like 2.5, ½, −3.7, and √2 are not integers. However, every integer can be written as a fraction (e.g., 5 = 5/1), which means all integers are rational numbers.

What are the different types of integers?

Integers are classified into three types: Positive integers (1, 2, 3, …) — greater than zero; Negative integers (−1, −2, −3, …) — less than zero; and Zero (0) — neither positive nor negative. Together these three types make up the complete set of integers ℤ.

Can a negative number be an integer?

Yes. Negative integers such as −1, −2, −3, −100 are all integers. A number only fails to be an integer if it has a fractional or decimal part. The sign (positive or negative) has nothing to do with whether a number is an integer.

What are consecutive integers?

Consecutive integers are integers that follow each other in order with a difference of 1. For example: 4, 5, 6 or −3, −2, −1, 0. In algebra they are often written as n, n+1, n+2. Consecutive even or odd integers have a difference of 2 (e.g. 2, 4, 6 or 1, 3, 5).

What are the applications of integers?

Integers appear wherever quantities can go below zero: temperature (−5°C), floors in a building (basement = −1), bank balances (overdraft = negative), altitude above and below sea level, and gains/losses in sport scores. In computing, integers are the primary data type for counting and indexing.
