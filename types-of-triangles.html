Types of Triangles

By the end of this page, you'll classify any triangle by its sides and by its angles, understand all 6 types, and know which combinations are possible — and which are impossible.

Every triangle belongs to exactly one of six categories. Here's the two-system classification.

Think about it

A triangular road sign, a slice of pizza, and a sail on a boat are all triangles — but they look completely different. There are two independent classification systems: one based on side lengths, one based on angles. Every triangle gets one label from each.

Triangles are classified in two independent ways: by their side lengths (equilateral, isosceles, scalene) and by their angles (acute, right, obtuse). The one rule that holds for every triangle, always: the three interior angles sum to exactly 180°.

Where triangles appear
🏛️
Pyramids

The face of the Great Pyramid of Giza is an isosceles triangle — two equal slanted sides meeting at the apex.

🏗️
Roofs

House roof cross-sections are typically isosceles triangles — two equal slopes meeting at the ridge for a balanced structure.

🚦
Road Signs

Yield and warning signs are equilateral triangles — all sides equal, instantly recognisable from any angle.

🎸
Guitar Picks

Many guitar picks are scalene triangles — three different side lengths give distinct corners for different playing techniques.

Types of Triangles by Sides

Count the equal sides — that's all it takes to classify a triangle by its side lengths.

Rule
All 3 sides equal → all 3 angles = 60° → 3 lines of symmetry

Equal sides force equal angles. With three equal angles that sum to 180°, each must be exactly 60°. An equilateral triangle is always acute — it can never be right or obtuse.

Example
Side = 5 cm → all angles = 60°, perimeter = 15 cm
Key fact
Only triangle type where all three classification properties are forced by just one measurement.
Rule
Exactly 2 sides equal → base angles equal → 1 line of symmetry

The angles opposite the equal sides (base angles) are always equal — the Isosceles Triangle Theorem. An isosceles triangle can be acute, right, or obtuse depending on the vertex angle.

Vertex angle 40°
Base angles = (180°−40°)÷2 = 70° each
Vertex angle 90°
Base angles = 45° each → right isosceles
Rule
All 3 sides different → all 3 angles different → no lines of symmetry

No two sides are equal, so no two angles are equal. A scalene triangle can be acute, right, or obtuse. The 3-4-5 right triangle is the most famous scalene triangle.

Example (right scalene)
Sides 3, 4, 5 — all different, one angle = 90°
Example (obtuse scalene)
Angles 30°, 50°, 100° — all different, one > 90°

Types of Triangles by Angles

Look at the largest angle — that single angle determines the angle classification.

Rule
All 3 angles < 90°  |  All three corners are "sharp"

Every angle is strictly less than 90°. An equilateral triangle (60°-60°-60°) is always acute. A triangle can be acute and isosceles, acute and scalene, or acute and equilateral.

Examples
60°-60°-60° (equilateral acute) | 70°-70°-40° (isosceles acute) | 50°-70°-60° (scalene acute)
Test
Check the largest angle in the triangle — if it is < 90°, the triangle is acute.
Rule
One angle = 90°  |  The other two must be acute and sum to 90°

The side opposite the 90° angle is the hypotenuse — always the longest side. The other two sides are called legs. The Pythagorean theorem applies: a² + b² = c².

Right isosceles
90°-45°-45° | Two legs equal | used in square diagonals
Right scalene
90°-60°-30° | All sides different | 3-4-5 is the simplest
Mini-check

A triangle has angles 90°, 45°, 45°. What type is it by sides? (Hint: which sides must be equal?)

Rule
One angle > 90°  |  Only ONE can be obtuse (the others must be acute)

Once one angle exceeds 90°, the remaining two must sum to less than 90° — so both must be acute. Two obtuse angles would exceed 180°, which is impossible. An equilateral triangle can never be obtuse.

Obtuse isosceles
120°-30°-30° | The vertex angle is obtuse
Obtuse scalene
100°-50°-30° | All sides different, one obtuse angle

Key Takeaways

TermDefinition
EquilateralAll 3 sides equal, all 3 angles = 60°. Always acute. 3 lines of symmetry.
IsoscelesExactly 2 sides equal. Base angles equal. 1 line of symmetry. Can be acute, right, or obtuse.
ScaleneAll 3 sides different, all 3 angles different. No lines of symmetry. Can be any angle type.
AcuteAll 3 angles less than 90°. Every equilateral triangle is acute.
RightExactly one angle = 90°. The side opposite is the hypotenuse. Pythagorean theorem applies.
ObtuseExactly one angle greater than 90°. Only one obtuse angle is possible — the others must be acute.
Angle SumAll three interior angles of any triangle sum to exactly 180°.
Impossible comboObtuse equilateral — equilateral forces each angle = 60°, which can never exceed 90°.
